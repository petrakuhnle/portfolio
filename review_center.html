Review Center

Making Review Assignments easier

Getting an overview for Review Assignment and Completion with more assignment, view and filter options with an interconnected Review Center.

Review Center Valispace → Altium 365 Designed 2021 · Live 2026

01 · Context

Assigning reviews to specific employees or groups of employees can be time consuming. The integrated Review Center allows for better overview, faster assignments and stayd interconnected with the rest of the engineering project.

02 · From requirements to design

Review Center Requirements were writen based on input from experts and expert user input and my task was to co-write, edit and further specify high level feature requirements to specific design requriemnts and designs. What exactly does the person who assigns need to be able to select to the exact different selection options down to details of specific wording, UI behaviour, icons and colors. Therefore I was the main driver for discovery, mapping out the logic concrete enough to define and refine requirements and draft high fidelity and interactive Figma designs. Breaking down somewhat vague verbal descriptions of what is frustrating or what the user would need to be able to do or decide to concrete user stories and UI elements is a process that helps in discovery since the concrete and detailed level of a Figma prototypes will bring up questions that can be fed back to the user and expert interviews - this way we could iterate and refine to ensure enough use cases are covered for the feature to be useful.

03 · Process

01 · User journeys

The manager or employee responsible to assigning reviews may need to manage a lot of reviews. Who needs to review what by what time? Are the reviews done? Do the reviews lag behind too much to keep up with the timeline of the project? The user may need to assign similar people to similar requirements over and over again or not get a new overview considering that hardware engineering projects can easily involve 100s or 1000s of requirements and 100s of people. On top of it, reviews may need to be redone or changes can trickle down to the planning of reviews. In addition, the reviews or the communication about it may be tracked separate from other work - in another software and reviewed in meetings or asked about per e-mail.

04 · Key design decision

A key decision was to not only consider individual reviewers a user may want to assign but also groups of reviewers. Reviews repeat itself and were possible, the feature aims at reducing repeptition to reduce the time it costs to complete a tasks seen over a month time or in the context of one project, not only the time to complete one specific task in the software.

05 · Alignment across roles

The feature was aligned with experts and CPO in close collaboration with SME who stood in for user testing and clients who provided expert users for interviews and testing. The logic was mapped out by experts and me as designer and development took over the user stories and designs as a starting point to build the feature. One backend developer would provide the feedback to me as designer, that the logic was solid since it was easy to later integrate feedback into the existing logic. While it could have been beneficial to have a developer join the design and discovery, for this expert knowledge feature, the time it required would have been extensive and this was an ongoing consideration since it remained the main challagne to balance a feature where even the minimal version needs to be complex enough to be useful while also reducing the complexity enough to first build a feasible and small system-in-system's feature that works to later expand it. For me as designer, the work was highly logical and system's based rather than UI or visual polish, but I discovered that the questions I needed to ask in order to design the feature needed to be asked from the logic of the user and not yet considering the logic of the code. Therefore the combination of a complex design with the user logic as reference, with flexibiility of the development to find the best backend and frontend solution for it worked well. The process of interviewing, testing and designing the design logic was crucial in capturing the complexity before starting to code. Only testing and interviewing several expert users allowed as to generalize enough to know what to build later. Find priorities and agree on shortterm (MVP) and longterm version of the feature was crucial for development know how to write the code. This type of work on complex workflow designs does constantly bring up the question what is most crucial, what is too complex and who should be involved how early for discover, design and development. As lessons learned based on my experience, involving experts and experts user early in discovery and design was crucial. Also design being involved very early with first interviews, drafts of user stories and first sketches was the best approach to achieve a realistic and user friendly version. What we could have done better is to add in a middle part and adjust and feedback with the existing designs but consider development and build first code prototypes and not go straight to just code. All mentioned participants need to be involved and for a software for experts, like aerospace engineering, all roles need to acknowledge dependency on true collaboration between the roles to be succesful with the development of these types of features - which we proofed by not only releasing the features but the features living still today in Altium 365 after they acquired Valispace. Another question would be whether to design for an existing workflow, invent completely new ways of working etc. which is more a C-level or PM-level decision to make and depends on position of the company and where money flows and these can heavily impact the type of user a designer needs to speak to and / or how to collaborate with, how and when
