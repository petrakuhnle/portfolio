04 · Process · domain standards first

Discovery here meant learning the conventions before drawing anything.

I reviewed similar tools engineers used to understand the conventions they expect, then interviewed users on where those tools fail their day to day. Experts and I mapped the logic in the language of the user, not yet the logic of the code; development took the user stories and designs as the starting point and kept the flexibility to find the best solution.

Views resolve conventions, user needs and constraints

Methods & discovery

User journeys and user stories as perspective-taking — user-centred throughout, the user kept central. Artefacts: user stories and Figma prototypes. Early discovery with 2 external users/experts and 4 internal experts, some closely involved, others for interviews and tests.

Constraints · industry standards define what a review must record · integrate into the existing platform and data model · 1000s of requirements, 100s of people

05 · Key design decisions

The recurring question: how do formal-review conventions map onto data that already lives in the project? I designed the roles, states and views so a review is traceable without leaving the requirements.

Assign groups, not only individualsreviews repeat, so reduce the cost over a project, not one task
Rating per object, loggedthe approver closes the review with a timestamp
Views per rolefilters for specialists, requirement set and status for decision makers

06 · Lessons learned

Involve experts and expert users early, design earlier still.

What I'd add: a middle step of first code prototypes, feeding development constraints back into the designs before full implementation.

Crucial were the different views and filters per role. Improving the discovery would mean testing with different companies and different roles — hard in B2B, but crucial to avoid optimising within a narrow space. Not necessary for this MVP, but an important next step before adjusting to individual users' wishes.
