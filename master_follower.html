03 · How it works

One change on the Master. Every Follower flagged. The owner applies or rejects — logged.

A Master change reaches each Follower as its own state to review

The changes in the Master requirement propagate automatically. A key decision was to allow different states — synced, pending, rejected — so a change left pending too long or rejected can still be acted on. The feature also lets everyone involved communicate in place about the changes and decisions.

04 · Process · 0→1 in a new domain

I came into systems engineering as a non expert, and used that.

I could ask the questions a fellow engineer might assume instead of validate — so I built the feature logic from expert and user interviews, not documentation.

Hired as the first designer six years into an engineering-led startup, I introduced interviews and prototypes to a sketch → build approach — joining the conversations, starting with Figma prototypes, and gradually adding methods that helped the company define and align a feature before committing to it.

Before that, a wide gap sat between what engineers pictured and what developers understood to build — the jump from high-level concept to code was too big. This first systems-in-systems feature still spanned that gap; I closed it over time with more insistence on MVP scope and user stories. → Use Case 2

05 · Key design decisions

My job was to ask the specific questions: what does the engineer working on a Follower need to see, what would frustrate them, what does the Master's editor need versus the Follower's owner? Same user focus, different users in different scenarios — perspective taking. I build from the user and visual side so developers can focus on the technical side, and we meet in the middle.

Flags, not silent updatesthe owner of each Follower applies or rejects the change

06 · Lessons learned

Complexity, feasibility, and the value of the feedback loop.

A few months into my first product design job, I assumed the difficulty of the systems engineering logic was mine to absorb. It shipped, but was harder to build than we anticipated. On the next feature I asked for user-story collaboration and agreement on the most crucial requirements from the start. See Use Case 2, Verification Assignment Matrix.

Ideally there would have been a middle step — more user testing and detailed feasibility alignment, rather than going prototype → contract → build. Part startup reality, part outside my influence at the time.

This first, extensive feature was also my education in requirements and systems engineering. Because I had to design what I was told and show it back to the same experts a week later, I paid closer attention than listening alone allows — and later caught a detail that prevented a development refactor. The feedback loop is what makes you pay attention.
