Feature: Master-Follower

Probagating decisions to dependant requirements across 1000s of requirements with 100s of shared responsibiilities.

Between meetings, documents and emails decision makers and engineers can loose track of decisions that are ciritcal. A change in a master requirement propagates information automatically to Follower requirements.

[Feature Tag] Valispace → Altium 365 Designed 2021 · Live 2026

01 · Context

Managers, requirements engineer, verification engineer, and systems engineer work on the same projects and decisions go from changing legal requirements the engineer needs to know about to a manager signing off on all verification methods being completed. The interdependencies can slow down the process and interfere with tasks at hand. The engineering industry has moved from documents and meetings to software solutions, but is fundamentally different from software due to the costly consequence, physical constraints and more legal constraints. Decisions and reviews are more often mission-critical and need to be accountable and transparent compared to software development collaboration for example. The Master-Follower is aimed at defining high impact requirements (Master) and their dependant requirements (Follower).

02 · From requirements to design

For a design from scratch without existing written requirements, I start with formal and informal interviews. To anchor my understand and organize importance. If it is an unknown domain, the anchors are especially important. What is the biggest frustration? What is the day to day reality right now? Can you show me? What would you need to know first? As a first time designer without any previous exposure to systems or requirements engineering, I relied on my listening, interviewing and visualization skills that I had forged through my career.


I started writing down notes, later drafting user stories. Prototyping logic, wireframes and interactive prototypes in Figma. Since I had expert users and internal Subject Matter Experts as my users to ask and test with, I anchored into what they already knew and were comfortable with - realistic looking interactive designs. High fidelity interactive Figma prototypes. Asking specific questions and having people share their screens and go through short interactions at a time, iterating weekly. I started out with who was in front of me, expanding to people in the company with the relevant work experience to qualify as stand-in for an expert users of the software. Working with what I have and expanding from there.


At the start of my first systems-in-systems feature I drafted text and visuals in parallel, clearly separating the requirements (written user stories) from designs. Translating from written requirements to design requirements by breaking down further into smaller pieces, individual UI behaviours.

04 · Key design decision

A key decision of this feature was to ensure that at the moment of popagation of the decision on the master requirement to ensure that there is clarity around the propagation. My focus on User Experience enabled me to switch into different user roles for the questioning. I didn't need to know what an engineer needs to do in detail, I had to ask what an engineer who gets the notification of the propagation might want to know or decide at that moment. Perspective taking : Considering different types of users and asking about frustrations, edge cases and needs helped us design the feature in a way that wouldn't be aimed at satisfying one user but frustrating another.

05 · Alignment across roles

The alignment happened with weekly meetings, feedback-sessions, presentations adn e-mails that were partially planned and partially self-initiated to keep everyone aligned and integrate feedback. CPO, CTO, Solution management, Subject Matter Experts where involved in the main decision making. I was responsible for getting the information I needed, designing, iterating and updating everyone. I wasn't in charge of timelines and decising who else was involved in the project. The main challange was to make the feature complex enough to be useful in the highly specialized work context and simple enough to make it feasible. This was and is a specific and ongoing challenge for complex B2B software.

06 · Lessons Learned

Since I designed this complex systems-in-systems feature in my first product design jobs only a few months in, I was still learning while having to create designs. My modesty caused me to design a feature that was too complex thinking that it must be me who can hardly handle the complexity of a systems engineering logic in a an interactive Figma prototype. It was feasible, but less feasible as anticipated. But at the same time, a more feasible and less complex feature may not have been as useful to complex engineering projects. Learning from this project, I asked for user story collaboration and offical agreement on most crucial user requirements fromt he beginning, pushed more confidently and created a more limited yet complex feature where we spend significantly less time for development and design. -> See Case Study 2: Verification Assignment Feature
